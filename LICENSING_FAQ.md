# Synth Licensing FAQ

## What license is Synth released under?

Synth is released under the Business Source License 1.1 (BSL 1.1). This is a source-available license that permits free use, modification, and redistribution, with limited restrictions on specific commercial use cases. On the Change Date (March 16, 2030 for Synth 1.0), the license automatically converts to the MIT License.

## Can I use Synth for free?

Yes. You can use Synth in production at no cost. The self-hosted version is free and ungated — no registration, no license key, no usage limits. This applies to individuals, startups, and enterprises alike.

## What am I restricted from doing?

Two things:

1. **Offering Synth as a hosted or managed service** where the primary value of the service is Synth's functionality itself. In other words, you cannot resell Synth as a service.

2. **Embedding Synth into a competing product** — specifically, a product offered to third parties that uses AI or language models to plan, reason about, or execute operational changes to software deployments, infrastructure, or application environments.

Everything else is permitted.

## Can I use Synth internally at my company?

Yes, without restriction. Internal production use is fully permitted regardless of company size.

## Can our platform team run Synth for other teams in our organization?

Yes. Internal operation — even across teams, business units, or subsidiaries — is permitted.

## Can I modify Synth's source code?

Yes. You can modify, fork, and create derivative works. Modified versions are subject to the same BSL 1.1 terms.

## Can a consultant or MSP deploy Synth on our behalf?

Yes. A consultant, managed service provider, or systems integrator deploying Synth on your infrastructure, for your use, is permitted. The restriction applies to offering Synth itself as a service to third parties — not to helping a client deploy it.

## Can I include Synth in my product?

It depends on what your product does. If your product uses AI or language models to plan, reason about, or execute operational changes to deployments, infrastructure, or application environments, and you offer that product to third parties, the license does not permit this without a commercial agreement. If your product does something else and Synth is a supporting component (not the primary value), this is generally permitted. When in doubt, contact licensing@synthdeploy.com.

## Is BSL 1.1 open source?

No. BSL 1.1 is a source-available license, not an OSI-approved open source license. The source code is publicly available and you can use, modify, and redistribute it, but with the commercial restrictions described above. On the Change Date, the code converts to MIT, which is a fully open source license.

## What happens on the Change Date?

On March 16, 2030, Synth 1.0's license automatically converts from BSL 1.1 to the MIT License. At that point, all restrictions are removed and the code is fully open source. Each major version of Synth has its own Change Date.

## Does the BSL affect my data or configurations?

No. Your deployment configurations, operational data, and any content you create using Synth are yours. The license applies only to Synth's source code, not to your data.

## I need a commercial license. How do I get one?

Contact licensing@synthdeploy.com. Commercial licenses are available for use cases that fall outside the Additional Use Grant.
